Bringing the Divine Into Your Marriage: The Missing Partner

How to Transform Your Relationship by Inviting Hashem In • 7 min read

The Contradiction That Changes Everything

I'm going to tell you something that might seem contradictory at first, but understanding it will transform your marriage—and your life.

On one hand, I've written about commitment and putting in hard work to build a strong marriage. Marriage requires serious effort, difficult choices, and constant dedication.

But I've also said that it takes a miracle for a man and woman to live together in a happy, peaceful home.

So which is it? Hard work or a miracle?

The answer is both—and that's the key to everything.

You need to understand this answer, believe it, internalize it, and then live it. Doing this will change your marriage completely.

The answer is that there's actually a third partner in your home—if you let Him in. This third partner brings with Him peace, love, and happiness. He wants to come, but you have to want Him there too.

That third partner is Hashem.

The Partnership You Never Knew You Needed

Here's what most couples don't realize: Hashem is the one who split the sea to bring every husband and wife together. You only stood under the chuppah with your spouse because Hashem orchestrated every detail to put you there, together.

Hashem will provide the miracle you need, but you need to do the hard work to make Him feel welcome.

The Most Powerful Tool: Prayer

Once you've established Hashem and His will as the foundation of your home, the first and strongest practical step you can take is prayer.

I cannot overemphasize how important this is.

Every single day, you should ask Hashem for His help to bring shalom bayis. Make your prayers personal for your specific circumstances.

Why Every Marriage Hits the Wall (And How to Break Through)

Turning Crisis Into Breakthrough • 5 min read

Every couple faces this moment. You know the one I'm talking about.

The moment when you look at your spouse and think, "This isn't what I signed up for." When the romance has faded, the honeymoon is over, and you're left staring at this person across the breakfast table wondering how you're going to make this work for the next 50 years.

Welcome to the wall. Every marriage hits it.

But here's what most people don't realize: hitting the wall isn't the end of your marriage story. It's actually the beginning of your real marriage.

Why the Wall Exists

The wall exists because infatuation isn't love. Those early feelings—the butterflies, the constant thinking about each other, the way everything they did seemed perfect—that wasn't actually love. That was attraction mixed with projection mixed with hope.

Real love can only begin when you see your spouse clearly. And that requires the illusions to fall away.

The wall is where illusions go to die. And that's exactly what needs to happen.

The Breakthrough: Choosing to Build

The breakthrough happens when you make a fundamental shift in perspective:

You stop asking "Is this person right for me?" and start asking "How can we build something beautiful together?"

You stop trying to change your spouse and start working on becoming the person your spouse needs you to be.

You stop waiting for feelings to carry your marriage and start choosing actions that create connection.

What's on the Other Side

Here's what happens when you break through the wall instead of breaking up:

You discover that real love—the kind that lasts—isn't based on butterflies or perfection. It's based on choice, commitment, and the deep satisfaction that comes from building something meaningful together.

The couples who break through don't just survive the wall. They build their strongest foundation on the other side.

How to Fight Without Destroying Your Marriage: A Torah Approach

Conflict is Inevitable. Damage is Optional. • 6 min read

Let me start with something that might surprise you: Good marriages have conflict.

The couples who claim they "never fight" usually fall into one of two categories: they're either lying, or one person has completely given up their voice in the relationship. Neither is healthy.

Conflict isn't the enemy of marriage. Destructive conflict is.

The Torah gives us a framework for handling conflict that protects relationships while still allowing for honest disagreement. Here's how to fight without destroying what you've built together.

The Foundation: Remembering Your Goal

Before we talk about technique, we need to establish the right mindset. When conflict arises, most couples unconsciously shift into "me vs. you" mode. They're trying to win.

But in marriage, if one person wins and the other loses, you both lose.

The Torah approach starts with remembering that you're on the same team. Your goal isn't to win the argument—it's to strengthen your relationship while addressing the issue at hand.

The Torah's Rules of Engagement

The Torah gives us specific guidelines for how to handle conflict constructively:

Rule #1: Go Direct (Hocheach Tochiach)

Don't talk to everyone except the person you have an issue with. Don't drop hints and hope they'll figure it out. Address issues directly with your spouse.

"I need to talk to you about something that's bothering me. When would be a good time?"

Rule #2: Don't Embarrass (Lo Talbinu)

Never bring up your spouse's faults in public. Don't use their vulnerabilities as weapons. Keep your conflicts private and protect your spouse's dignity.

Rule #3: Watch Your Words (Shemiras Halashon)

Words have power. Once said, they can't be unsaid. Before you speak, ask yourself: "Will these words help us resolve this issue, or will they just cause more damage?"

The Safe Fighting Framework

Here's a practical framework for having difficult conversations without causing damage:

Step 1: State the Issue, Not the Person
"I'm frustrated that the kitchen wasn't cleaned" instead of "You're so messy."

Step 2: Share Your Experience
"When I come home to a messy kitchen, I feel overwhelmed" instead of "You don't care about our home."

Step 3: Ask for Understanding
"Can you help me understand your perspective?" instead of assuming you know their motivations.

Step 4: Work Together on Solutions
"How can we handle this better going forward?" instead of just rehashing the past.

The Long View

Remember: you're not just resolving today's conflict. You're establishing patterns for how you'll handle disagreements for the rest of your marriage.

Every time you choose to fight constructively instead of destructively, you're building trust. You're showing your spouse that they're safe with you, even when you disagree.

That safety is the foundation of everything good in marriage.

Conflict is inevitable. But damage is optional. Choose to fight for your marriage, not against each other.
